Global Market Overview

The global horse racing industry generates approximately $115 billion in total economic activity in 2026, making it one of the largest organized sporting markets worldwide. This figure encompasses wagering handle ($95 billion), breeding and bloodstock sales ($8.2 billion), racecourse operations and hospitality ($5.5 billion), media rights ($3.1 billion), and associated employment, tourism, and agricultural activity.

The industry's geographic footprint spans six continents, with major racing jurisdictions in the United States, United Kingdom, Ireland, France, Japan, Hong Kong, Australia, Dubai, and an expanding presence in Saudi Arabia, India, and South Korea. Japan's JRA handles more wagering volume than any other single racing organization, with annual handle exceeding $25 billion.

Wagering Revenue and Digital Transformation

Wagering is the economic engine of horse racing. The global trend toward online and mobile wagering has transformed how bettors interact with racing, and this digital shift continues to accelerate in 2026.

In the United States, advance deposit wagering (ADW) platforms -- online and mobile betting -- now account for approximately 68% of total thoroughbred handle, up from 52% in 2019. Traditional on-track wagering continues to decline, representing less than 15% of US handle. The remaining share comes from simulcast wagering at off-track facilities.

  • Fixed-odds horse racing betting becoming legal in 10+ US states by 2028: Priced at 45% YES. New Jersey legalized fixed-odds in 2023, and other states are following.

Internationally, the wagering landscape varies significantly. Hong Kong's handle (approximately $14 billion annually from a population of 7.5 million) demonstrates the revenue potential of well-regulated racing. Japan's handle has been growing steadily through digital platforms. Australian wagering continues to shift toward corporate bookmakers. The UK market has been relatively flat, with fixed-odds betting terminals (FOBTs) restrictions and regulatory changes creating headwinds.

Attendance Trends and Fan Engagement

Attendance at horse racing events presents a bifurcated picture. Premium events and festivals continue to thrive, while regular race day attendance declines across most jurisdictions.

Major events performing well: The Kentucky Derby regularly attracts 150,000+ attendees. Royal Ascot draws approximately 300,000 across the five-day meeting. The Melbourne Cup carnival attracts 350,000+. The Breeders' Cup has seen growing attendance since its two-day format was established. These events have successfully positioned themselves as social and hospitality occasions that transcend pure sporting interest.

Regular race day challenges: Day-to-day attendance at most racetracks has declined steadily. In the US, total annual racetrack attendance (excluding major events) has decreased approximately 15% since 2019. The average age of racetrack attendees continues to rise, and the industry has struggled to attract younger demographics to regular race meetings.

The industry is responding with several strategies: enhanced hospitality and social experiences, live music and entertainment integration, food and drink festivals coinciding with race days, and digital engagement tools that connect online bettors with the live racing experience. Prediction markets price at least one US racetrack launching a full entertainment district concept (combining racing, dining, shopping, and entertainment) by 2028 at 55% probability.

Regulatory Evolution: HISA and Beyond

The Horseracing Integrity and Safety Authority (HISA) represents the most significant regulatory change in US horse racing in decades. Established by federal legislation, HISA implements uniform anti-doping, medication control, and racetrack safety standards nationwide, replacing the previous patchwork of state-by-state regulation.

HISA's implementation has been contentious. Supporters argue that uniform standards protect horses and improve the sport's integrity. Critics raise concerns about federal overreach, implementation costs, and the impact on smaller racing operations. Legal challenges have tested HISA's authority, though the program continues to operate.

  • All major US racing jurisdictions fully compliant with HISA standards by 2028: Priced at 60% YES
  • HISA leading to measurable reduction in racing fatalities by 2028: Priced at 55% YES

Breeding Industry and Bloodstock Market

The thoroughbred breeding industry is valued at approximately $8.2 billion globally in 2026, encompassing stallion fees, broodmare sales, yearling auctions, and foal sales. The global thoroughbred foal crop has stabilized at approximately 90,000 annually after decades of decline from a peak of approximately 150,000 in the 1980s.

The elite end of the bloodstock market has been performing exceptionally well. The Keeneland September Yearling Sale grossed over $450 million in 2025, and the Tattersalls October Sale in the UK set records. Premium yearlings by fashionable stallions regularly sell for $1 million or more. This top-end strength contrasts with a challenging market for middle and lower-tier horses, creating a widening gap in bloodstock economics.

International Market Growth

While mature racing markets in the US and UK face structural challenges, several international markets are experiencing significant growth:

Saudi Arabia: The Saudi Cup, established in 2020 with the world's richest purse ($20 million), has rapidly elevated Saudi Arabia's position in global racing. The kingdom's investment in racing infrastructure, breeding operations, and international bloodstock purchases signals long-term commitment to becoming a major racing jurisdiction. Prediction markets price Saudi Arabia establishing a year-round domestic racing program by 2028 at 50% probability.

Japan: JRA handle continues to grow, driven by digital wagering platforms and the popularity of racing among younger demographics -- a success story that other jurisdictions study. Japan's domestic breeding industry has also improved dramatically, with Japanese-bred horses increasingly competitive in international Group 1 races.

India: With a population of 1.4 billion and a historical connection to horse racing, India represents potentially the largest untapped racing market. Racing operates in several cities (Mumbai, Bangalore, Hyderabad, Chennai), but regulatory uncertainty and infrastructure limitations have constrained growth. Prediction markets price India's racing handle doubling by 2030 at 35% probability.

Animal Welfare and Public Perception

Animal welfare is arguably the most significant long-term risk factor for the horse racing industry. High-profile incidents -- breakdowns during major televised races, doping scandals, and post-career horse welfare concerns -- generate negative public perception and political pressure.

The industry has responded with significant investments in safety: synthetic and cushioned track surfaces, pre-race veterinary examinations, injury databases, after-care programs for retired racehorses, and stricter medication rules through HISA and equivalent programs internationally. These measures have produced measurable results -- US racing fatality rates have decreased approximately 30% over the past decade.

Prediction markets track welfare-related developments:

  • US racing fatality rate declining by another 20% by 2028: Priced at 50% YES
  • Major racing jurisdiction banning whip use entirely by 2029: Priced at 25% YES
  • Universal retirement/after-care tracking for all thoroughbreds by 2029: Priced at 40% YES

Frequently Asked Questions

How big is the global horse racing industry in 2026?

The global horse racing industry is valued at approximately $115 billion in 2026, encompassing wagering handle, purse money, breeding, bloodstock sales, racecourse operations, media rights, and associated tourism. The wagering handle alone accounts for approximately $95 billion globally. The United States represents the largest single market at approximately $12 billion in total handle, followed by the UK/Ireland, Japan, Hong Kong, and Australia. Japan's JRA (Japan Racing Association) is the single largest racing organization by wagering volume.

Is horse racing attendance growing or declining?

Horse racing attendance trends are mixed. Major events like the Kentucky Derby, Royal Ascot, Melbourne Cup, and Breeders' Cup continue to attract record or near-record attendance. However, day-to-day attendance at regular race meetings has been declining across most major racing jurisdictions. In the US, total annual racetrack attendance has decreased approximately 15% since 2019. The industry is adapting by focusing on premium event experiences, hospitality packages, and digital engagement to offset the decline in casual attendees.

How will sports betting legalization affect horse racing?

Sports betting legalization across the United States has had a complex impact on horse racing. On one hand, new sportsbook operators have attracted some bettors away from horse racing toward easier-to-understand sports wagers. On the other hand, the normalization of legal betting has expanded the overall wagering market and brought new attention to all forms of betting including horse racing. Prediction markets price US horse racing handle growing modestly at 2-3% annually through 2030, with the growth coming primarily from online wagering platforms like FanDuel Racing and DraftKings.

What regulatory changes are expected in horse racing by 2028?

The Horseracing Integrity and Safety Authority (HISA) has been the most significant US regulatory development, implementing uniform anti-doping and safety standards nationwide. Prediction markets price HISA achieving full operational stability by 2027 at 65% probability. Additional expected regulatory changes include expanded whip use restrictions, enhanced post-race testing protocols, stricter medication rules, and increased transparency in ownership structures. Internationally, the trend toward centralized national regulation is expected to continue.

Will horse racing survive long-term as a major sport?

Prediction markets price horse racing remaining a major global sport through 2035 at 80%+ probability, though with significant structural changes. The sport will increasingly concentrate around premium events and major racing festivals, with smaller tracks facing economic pressure. Digital wagering will become the dominant revenue channel. International markets, particularly in Asia and the Middle East, will grow in importance. The key challenges are animal welfare concerns, competition from other betting products, and the aging demographics of the core fanbase.
